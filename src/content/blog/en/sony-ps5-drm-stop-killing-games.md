I recently came across some news in the gaming space that really left me scratching my head—and honestly, feeling quite powerless as a gamer in the digital age.

Here's the context: A while back, an amazing Twitter (X) account called [@DoesItPlay1](https://twitter.com/DoesItPlay1)—who specializes in testing whether games can actually be played offline—dropped a bombshell. They found that Sony supposedly sneaked a brutally strict new DRM (Digital Rights Management) mechanism into digital PS5 games, requiring your console to "check in" online every 30 days. Fail to do so, and you get locked out of the game you paid for.

My immediate reaction when I saw this was just a heavy sigh. *"Here we go again,"* I thought. *"When will the games we buy actually belong to us?"*

Now, major outlets like [IGN](https://www.ign.com/articles/playstation-addresses-ps5-drm-concerns-in-new-statement) and [GameSpot](https://www.gamespot.com/articles/playstation-users-report-new-online-license-checks/1100-6539651/) quickly got in touch with PlayStation, and the official PR response cooled things down—claiming it was just a "false alarm." Sony explained that this isn't some permanent new policy. Instead, it's just a temporary anti-fraud measure that kicks in during a game's refund eligibility window (usually 14 to 30 days). Once you pass the period where you can request a refund, the online verification timer vanishes, and you get your permanent offline license.

But to be completely honest with you? That "clarification" didn't make me feel better. If anything, it opened my eyes to a much deeper issue.

## Is "Anti-Fraud" Just a Convenient Excuse?

The moment I saw them using "refund protection" as their justification, I immediately thought about Steam. 

Steam is the absolute titan of PC gaming platforms, and everyone knows their refund policy is famously consumer-friendly: played for less than two hours and owned for less than 14 days? You get a refund, no questions asked. Steam processes an astronomical number of refunds every single day, and they manage it seamlessly just by checking account history and backend data.
Have they ever forced players who buy single-player games to deal with a ticking 30-day online time bomb just to "prevent refund fraud"? Never.

So why is it that a basic customer service issue—one that Steam handles effortlessly with backend algorithms—suddenly becomes an excuse for Sony to hold every legitimate digital buyer hostage with mandatory online check-ins? It’s genuinely hard for me not to suspect that "refund protection" is just a smokescreen. It feels like they're testing the waters, figuring out exactly how much aggressive DRM they can normalize before the community completely loses it.

## The Boiling Frog Effect: Losing Our Last Shred of Ownership

If you are a veteran of the PS4 and Xbox One console wars, you definitely remember how Sony aggressively mocked Microsoft.

Microsoft tried to push a mind-bogglingly strict DRM policy that required the Xbox One to connect to the internet every 24 hours, and the internet rightfully roasted them alive.

What did Sony do?

Sony released a deeply savage, brilliantly simple tutorial video titled "How to Share Your PS4 Games," where two guys just smiled and handed a physical disc to each other—specifically to mock Microsoft's DRM policy.

Now look at where we are.

The exact same company that claimed the moral high ground and mocked their rival is now quietly stuffing a remarkably similar "30-day online validation" feature into the PS5.

Even if they claim it’s just for refunds, it’s beyond hypocritical. It feels like a slap in the face to everyone who cheered for them back then.

Let's face facts: in the digital distribution era, software publishers are deliberately stripping away our actual ownership.
When we buy a digital game, we aren't "owning" a product anymore; we are just renting a fragile "license to use" that can be revoked at any second.
Publishers hold all the power. They can shut down servers, let music licenses expire, or just pull the plug on an older game to force you into buying the sequel.
And this is exactly why the 30-day DRM rumor sparked instant outrage. We didn't even pause to question if it was true—we just got incredibly angry, because we all know deep down that this is *exactly* the kind of anti-consumer stunt these multinational corporations would pull.

## This is Why I Support "Stop Killing Games"

For me, this entire fiasco is the perfect real-world example of why we desperately need the [Stop Killing Games](https://www.stopkillinggames.com/) movement.

The core message of the movement is straightforward and completely reasonable: when a developer decides to stop supporting a game, they have a responsibility to leave it in a playable state. They shouldn't be allowed to weaponize DRM or server checks to turn a game we paid for into dead digital trash. 

Sony’s 30-day "protection" mechanism proves one terrifying technical truth: publishers already have a built-in "kill switch" for our digital libraries.

If some high-ranking executive decides tomorrow that forcing permanent online DRMs or locking games behind subscriptions looks better for their quarterly earnings, we are completely defenseless. 

As we move deeper into an industry that desperately wants everything to be digital-only, we cannot keep relying on the "goodwill" or PR statements of corporations. The only way to stop them from taking away the games we bought is to support clear, legal frameworks that protect consumer rights and guarantee digital ownership. That’s our last line of defense as gamers.
