## What Is Stop Killing Games?

**Stop Killing Games** is a consumer rights movement initiated by the gaming community, built around a single core argument: **game companies should not have the right to render a purchased game completely unplayable after shutting down its servers.**

The movement was spearheaded by YouTuber **Ross Scott**, best known for his *Freeman's Mind* video series. He began actively pushing this issue in 2024, sparked by Ubisoft's announcement that it would delist *The Crew* and permanently shut down its servers — making the game completely unlaunchable for all buyers, including its solo content.

---

## Why Is This a Problem?

Many modern online multiplayer games — and even some single-player titles — require a constant connection to the developer's servers just to launch or play. Once a company decides to "shut down," whether due to financial difficulties, acquisition, or a plain business decision, every player's copy of the game instantly becomes a useless brick.

What makes this especially frustrating:

- Players **paid real money** for these games
- Companies typically **offer no refunds** before shutting down
- After shutdown, players are left with **no recourse whatsoever**
- Game companies can unilaterally decide when to pull the plug

This directly contradicts the traditional expectations of consumer ownership. If you buy a physical DVD and the studio shuts down, you can still watch it. But with today's digital games, that's no longer guaranteed.

---

## The Crew Incident

*The Crew* is an open-world racing game published by Ubisoft in 2014. It required a persistent internet connection to play. In March 2024, Ubisoft shut down its servers for good — rendering the game completely unlaunchable for all owners, with no offline fallback whatsoever.

This was far from the first game to be "killed" this way, but the scale of *The Crew* incident and the media attention it drew made it a turning point. Ross Scott used it as his flagship example to communicate the severity of the problem to the public and began pushing for legislative action.

---

## What the Movement Demands

Stop Killing Games is **not** asking for games to be supported indefinitely or for multiplayer servers to run forever. The core demand is:

> **When a game company ends support for a title, they must ensure the game remains playable without depending on their servers.**

Practical solutions could include:

- Releasing an **offline patch** or **local server mode**
- Allowing the community to host **private servers**
- Open-sourcing the **server-side code** for players to use
- **Refunding** all purchasers before shutdown

## The European Citizens' Initiative (ECI) & Parliamentary Hearing

Ross Scott took the movement to the policy level by launching a **European Citizens' Initiative (ECI)** in 2024, formally titled **"Stop Destroying Videogames"**.

The ECI is a direct democracy mechanism in the EU: if a proposal collects **1 million valid signatures from at least 7 EU member states** within the required timeframe, the European Commission is legally obligated to formally respond and consider legislation. This threshold was successfully met.

### 🏛️ April 16, 2026: Entering the European Parliament

On April 16, 2026, the movement reached a landmark milestone — **Ross Scott and campaign organizer Moritz Katzner appeared in person before the European Parliament** at a joint public hearing held by three committees:

- **IMCO**: Committee on the Internal Market and Consumer Protection
- **JURI**: Committee on Legal Affairs
- **PETI**: Committee on Petitions

The hearing was fully open to the public and streamed live. Ross Scott and Katzner presented the initiative's case to Members of the European Parliament (MEPs) and representatives of the European Commission, outlining the severity of games being killed by server shutdowns and proposing practical legislative remedies.

Following the hearing, the initiative entered the **formal European Commission review phase**. The Commission is required to **submit an official written response by July 27, 2026**, stating its intended actions or position.

## How the Industry Has Responded

The gaming industry has largely remained silent or resistant, citing reasons such as:

- **Financial and operational costs** of maintaining legacy server infrastructure
- Potential **intellectual property issues** from open-sourcing server code
- Technical complexity making offline modes "infeasible" for certain titles

However, critics point out that these are not compelling excuses. Numerous indie developers and smaller studios have already provided server tools or offline support for their games — proof that this is technically achievable when there's will to do so.

## Why Gamers Should Care

Even if the games you currently own aren't affected, this trend is accelerating. More and more games are adopting "Always Online" architectures — including single-player titles that have no genuine need for it.

If consumers don't speak up now, digital "ownership" risks becoming entirely nominal in the future: you paid for a product, but you never truly own it.

## A Broader Digital Rights Issue

Stop Killing Games is not merely a protest against game companies — it raises a fundamental question for the digital age:

> **In the era of digital distribution, what does it mean to "buy" something?**

If we allow corporations to silently render products inoperable after ending support, then what we're purchasing is not ownership — it's a **revocable license**. This principle extends far beyond gaming to e-books, music, software, and any digital good sold under similar terms.

The outcome of this movement could set a meaningful precedent for how digital consumer rights are defined and protected in the years ahead.
